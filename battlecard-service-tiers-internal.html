Service Tiers: Internal Sales Playbook & Pricing Reference

Bundle pricing, à la carte SKUs, composition rationale, and expansion motion for Akka sales teams
April 2026  |  Confidential — Akka Sales Use Only
Always Sell the Bundle First.

Bundles are the primary commercial unit. Every new-logo conversation starts with a bundle recommendation based on the customer's workload and tier fit. À la carte SKUs exist only for expansion and growth within a tier that the customer is already on. Never quote à la carte pricing on a new-logo deal. Never assemble a bundle from à la carte SKUs "because the customer asked." Bundles carry 33–44% built-in discounts off list — that discount is the reason bundles win. Mixing motions erodes the margin story and trains customers to negotiate against list.

Every bundle is engineered to match the minimum viable configuration a customer on that tier will actually need. Do not modify bundle compositions on the fly. If a customer needs more than the bundle includes, the right motion is à la carte expansion on top of the bundle, not bundle redesign.

À La Carte Guardrails — Do Not Quote on New-Logo Deals

  • Sandbox has no à la carte SKUs at all. It is a bundle-only SKU. If a Sandbox customer needs more service cores, they buy them at Day 2 Ops rates (1-core or 25-core).
  • Starter is consumption-priced — there is no "add a region" SKU. Starter customers scale by usage or upgrade to Day 2 Ops when they need BYOC.
  • BC and Sovereign have no 1-core SKU. Expansion at those tiers uses the 25-core or 100-core SKU only. This protects the commitment model.
  • TAM/FDE is always $400K regardless of tier. This is not negotiable at the SKU level.
  • If a customer asks you to quote à la carte before you've landed them on a bundle, redirect the conversation back to the bundle. Leading with à la carte always loses against bundle discount math.

Expansion Motion — How to Grow Within a Tier

Once a customer is on a bundle, à la carte SKUs are how you grow the account. The expansion playbook differs by tier. Memorize the natural expansion path for each tier before you land a new logo — the next conversation starts as soon as the first one closes.

From Starter
Starter customers expand through consumption — more instance-hours drive more monthly billing automatically. Watch for two signals: (1) monthly spend growing above ~$15K, (2) customer requesting BYOC. Both are upgrade signals to Day 2 Ops. Do not try to sell more Starter regions — the second region is already included. Position Day 2 Ops as "bring what you've built in our cloud into your own cloud, with production-grade guarantees."
From Sandbox
Sandbox customers have only one natural expansion motion: upgrade tiers. If they need more service cores, they technically can buy them at Day 2 Ops 1-core ($1,995) or 25-core ($45K) prices, but this is the wrong move commercially — it signals they've outgrown Sandbox. The right conversation is: "You're running more workload than Sandbox was designed for. Let's scope a Day 2 Ops bundle and plan the rebuild." Every Sandbox customer should graduate within 6–9 months or you're leaving money on the table.
From Day 2 Ops
Two expansion paths. (1) Add capacity within the tier: sell additional 25-core blocks ($45K each) as the customer's workload grows, or add a second 1/4 TAM ($100K for 1/4 time) if architecture support gaps emerge. (2) Upgrade the tier: when the customer's workload becomes revenue-bearing or customer-facing, the upgrade to Business Continuity is natural — lead with "what happens if this region fails?" Every Day 2 Ops deployment is a BC deal in waiting; track the upgrade trigger in the account plan.
From Business Continuity
BC customers expand through additional regions, additional cores, or additional TAM time. Additional regions at $150K each (BC pricing). Additional 100-core blocks at $100K each (volume rate kicks in at this tier). Additional TAM time at $100K per 1/4 time. Watch for multi-region sprawl — our largest customers span 20+ regions, and each new region is an expansion opportunity. The natural upgrade from BC is Sovereign, triggered by a data residency or regulatory requirement.
From Sovereign Cloud
Sovereign customers rarely upgrade beyond Sovereign — it is the top tier. Expansion happens via additional sovereign regions ($300K each) as the customer enters new regulated markets, and additional TAM allocations as local delivery teams come online in each sovereign region. The expansion story is geographic: "you're on Akka in the EU; let's scope Akka in Singapore and Japan with the same compliance posture."

When to Upgrade a Tier vs. Expand Within One

The commercial difference is significant: a tier upgrade is a new bundle sale (high ACV, high commit); within-tier expansion is an à la carte uplift (lower ACV, faster close). Choose the right motion based on the customer's trigger event.

Pricing Objection Handling — What to Say When Price Comes Up

"$175K for Day 2 Ops is too much — can you do $100K?"
Don't discount — explain the math. The bundle is already ~34% off list. At $175K you're saving them $90K vs à la carte. Reducing further erodes bundle integrity and trains the customer to push back on every renewal. If they genuinely can't afford Day 2 Ops, the right answer is Sandbox at $25K, with an explicit upgrade conversation planned for 6 months out. Never unbundle to hit a number.
"Can we start with Sandbox and get the Day 2 Ops features we need later?"
Only if "later" means "rebuild on Day 2 Ops later." Sandbox does not do HA/DR, does not do 24/7 SRE, does not do rolling updates, and is not SLA-backed. If the customer's ask can be answered with Sandbox, sell Sandbox. If the ask includes any production characteristic, sell Day 2 Ops. Don't try to split the difference.
"Why is Sovereign so much more expensive than BC?"
The delta is primarily the second full-time TAM (2x vs 1/2 TAM) and the country-isolation infrastructure (3x region cost at $300K vs $150K). The 44% discount on Sovereign is the deepest in the book because TAM value is so concentrated. Walk the customer through the à la carte math: $900K of regions + $100K of cores + $800K of TAMs = $1.8M list, bundled at $1M. They're saving $800K.
"We want to pay quarterly / monthly / on consumption."
Only Starter supports monthly/consumption billing. Everything else is annual prepaid. If the customer is committed to monthly billing on a production workload, sell them Starter. If they want Day 2 Ops pricing on a monthly cadence, that's an exception that requires VP Sales approval — do not commit to it on the call.
"Can we buy just the region and add cores later?"
Technically yes (à la carte region + 1-core SKU), but the bundle is always cheaper if they'll consume the included cores over the year. Show them the bundle math: "you'd pay $60K for the region plus whatever cores you add à la carte. The bundle is $175K for 2 regions and 25 cores. Buy fewer than 25 cores à la carte and you're already over the bundle price." Let the math make the decision.
"The customer has no budget for Day 2 Ops this year."
Two moves. (1) Sell Sandbox for $25K this year, land the relationship, plan the Day 2 Ops upgrade for next fiscal. (2) Sell Starter with consumption billing — the monthly cadence fits opex budgets that can't absorb annual prepays. Both keep Akka in the account and set up a bigger deal in 6–12 months.

Pricing Discipline Rules — Non-Negotiable

  • Never quote à la carte pricing before a bundle. Leading with SKU math trains customers to negotiate against list, not against bundle value.
  • Never unbundle a bundle to hit a target price. If the customer can't afford the bundle, sell them a lower tier — not a de-composed version of a higher tier.
  • Never discount a bundle below its published price without VP Sales approval. The 33–44% discount is already in the bundle. Further discounting requires written exception.
  • Never promise Sandbox-to-higher-tier in-place upgrade. It's a rebuild. Any commitment otherwise creates a customer-success problem later.
  • Never quote TAM/FDE below $400K. TAM pricing is flat across tiers for a reason — it's the one SKU customers anchor on across deals.
  • Never let the customer assemble a bundle from à la carte SKUs. "Can you just put together X, Y, Z for me?" is a trap — redirect to the nearest bundle.
